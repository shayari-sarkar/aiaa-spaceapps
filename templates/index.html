James Webb Space Telescope

As we breathe life into JWST by using music, we believe it will spark curiosity
and excitement about space for the general public, transforming their perception from
merely an instrument in space to the culmination of generations of curiosity about
our place in the cosmos. JWST represents an agent to discover beyond the
confines of Earth-based observation, which is limited by light pollution that blocks
the few wavelengths that make it past Earth's atmosphere. Not only that, the
atmosphere distorts the light from celestial objects which blurs images and makes
them lower resolution. However, JWST has evolved in its capabilities, as it reaches
further and further back in time to answer the most fundamental questions: where
do we come from, where are we headed, and what is our place and uniqueness in
comparison to the rest of the Universe.

IC 5332

The Rayleigh-Jeans Law states that the amount of electromagnetic energy
released from an object can be calculated using the object’s temperature. The
spiral galaxy IC 5332 is able to be captured in unprecedented detail thanks to
James Webb Space Telescope’s Mid-InfraRed Instrument (MIRI), which reveals
that the galaxy’s symmetrical spiral arms emit infrared light at a variety of temperatures.
3 / 6

Wolf-Rayet 124

Einstein’s Mass-Energy Equivalence equation states that the mass of an object
determines how much energy it can produce. This is true for some types of
supernova (explosion of a star), where the energy of the explosion increases with
the mass of the star. This image displays the structure of WR 124’s nebula, which
is made from material given off of aging stars in random ejections. This
destruction causes the star to lose mass, and leads to its eventual collapse.
4 / 6

Arp 107

The Planck-Einstein Relation is a formula integral to quantum mechanics which
relates the energy of a photon (particle of light) to its frequency. In this JWST
image, near-infrared data, shown in white, represents older stars, while young
stars and star-forming regions are shown in vibrant orange and red. Information
about the star formation taking place in these two galaxies and how they collided
millions of years ago can be collected by this composite image.
5 / 6


IC 1623

Schrödinger’s Equation can be used to describe the long-term distortion
and evolution of astrophysical disks, which form from gravitational forces when huge
volumes of rock, ice and space debris orbit a massive astronomical object. A black
hole is an example of a massive astronomical object, and this image shows a pair
of interacting galaxies that could eventually form into one. The collision creates a
rapid state of star formation known as a starburst, as featured in this JWST image.
6 / 6

NGC 5468

Hubble’s Law expresses the rate at which the Universe is expanding. NGC 5468,
shown in this image, is the most distant galaxy that has been identified to contain
Cepheid variable stars, which are important markers for measuring the expansion
rate of our Universe.
